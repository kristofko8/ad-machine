# Deep Research Methodology

Source: Mark Builds Brands — Research Part 1 & 2

## Why Research

The market will tell you what it wants to hear, what it wants you to write about — and in their own language. You're not writing what you think they want. You're writing what they told you they want. Copy-paste their own words into your copy.

---

## Section 1: Demographic Insights

### Who is your customer
- Age, gender, income, location
- Basic demographic data

### What attitudes do they hold
- **Religious** — if the market is predominantly Christian, adjust language accordingly. If they're atheists/biohackers, different approach.
- **Political** — conservative vs. liberal, language changes dramatically
- **Social** — views on education, role of government, social norms
- **Economic** — savers vs. spenders, retirees, attitude toward the economy

Goal: Be a "familiar voice" — someone they know and trust, because we speak their language.

### Hopes and dreams
Not just related to the product, but in life. Want to retire at 50? Live to 100? Start a business? Specific dreams, not generic "want to be healthy."

### Wins and failures
Where they've been successful with their problem and where they've failed. Especially failures — those are gold for copy.

### External forces preventing them from living their best life
Stories they tell themselves: "Wall Street is rigged," "Big pharma is corrupt," "If only I had gone to college." People have narratives about why they're not where they want to be. Capture the consistent ones.

### Prejudices
Not racial — but stereotypes and beliefs. "Italian women cook the best," "Influencers in yoga pants don't get it." If you reflect this in copy, you signal "I'm one of you" and build rapport.

### Core beliefs about life, love, and family
Summarize in 1-3 sentences who this person is. E.g.: "They're typically 45-65, believe the economy is rigged and the political system doesn't work, but family is everything. Marriage is sacred and the greatest gift is being a loving parent."

## Section 2: Existing Solutions

### What the market is already using
List everything — supplements, diets, coaches, courses, programs, apps...

### What was their experience
Do they use it a lot? A little? Did they stop? Why?

### What they like about existing solutions
Fast, simple, got results, manageable even for older people...

### What they don't like
Hard, hungry diets, side effects, doesn't work long-term...

**Why we want to know this:** When selling our product, we want to:
1. Show we have what they like about other solutions
2. Differentiate from what they don't like
3. Answer objections like "is this really different from what I've already tried?"

### Horror stories about existing solutions
True stories where something went terribly wrong. They make very persuasive copy and help discredit competing solutions.

### Does the market believe existing solutions work?
- If YES → you have more work on differentiation
- If NO → why? What prejudices and experiences influence this?

## Section 3: Curiosity

### Has anyone tried to solve the market's pain points in a unique way in the past?
Look for:
- **Lost discoveries** — solutions that were forgotten
- **Suppressed solutions** — someone had the answer but it was hidden/buried
- **Old unique attempts** — pre-1960, inherently different from today's solutions

People love the concept of "what's old is new again" and "ancient wisdom."

### Examples:
- **Tesla and energy** — Edison and GE didn't want him to share his discoveries, he was discredited. If you're selling a solar product, connect it to Tesla.
- **Foot fungus and WW2** — soldiers had massive foot fungus problems, government searched for a solution, discovered undecylenic acid. "The forgotten cure that helped the USA win WW2."

### Old attempts — what happened?
Were they successful but forgotten? Failed and why?

## Section 4: Corruption (Fall from Eden)

### Did the market's pain point always exist, or did it get worse?
"Fall from Eden" approach — there used to be simpler times, now it's worse because of an external corrupting force.

### Example: Obesity and Dr. Ancel Keys
1948 — Eisenhower had a stroke. Dr. Keys declared fat as the enemy. The entire food industry changed. Result: 70+ years of bad diet, diabetes/obesity/cancer skyrocketed. The food industry has an interest in maintaining the status quo.

### Isolated groups
Anywhere you find an isolated group of people who DON'T have the problem we have — why? Connect it to the corruption of modern society.

### Google searches:
- "[problem] horror story"
- "popular [solutions] from the 1900s"
- "places where [problem] doesn't exist"

## Where to Find Research

### 1. Forums
- Search "[topic] forum" on Google
- Open threads with the most replies and views
- Sort by replies = most engaged topics
- Views = potential subject lines for emails
- **Copy-paste exact quotes** in the market's language
- You don't have to go in order — when you find gold, extract and categorize

### 2. Amazon Reviews
- Look for products with lots of reviews
- **5-star reviews** = what they like, what results they got, why it works
- **1-star reviews** = what they don't like, horror stories, disappointments
- Copy-paste customer stories

### 3. Google
- Horror stories, corruption angles, old solutions, demographic data
- "[problem] horror story"
- "popular [solutions] history"
- "places where [problem] doesn't exist"
- SimilarWeb for competitor demographic data

---

## Key Principles

1. **The market writes your copy** — don't guess what they want to hear, they'll tell you
2. **Their language, not yours** — copy-paste exact words and phrases
3. **Hopes & dreams are not generic** — not "I want to lose weight" but "I want my husband to look at me with pride"
4. **The average American reads at a 7th grade level** — simplify language
5. **One good forum thread = most of your research** — when you find a gold mine, extract everything
6. **2-3 hours of research is enough** — when your wheels are spinning and you have enough material, stop
